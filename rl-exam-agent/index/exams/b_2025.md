## Q1 (30 pts) â€” Finite-horizon control on the Boolean hypercube
**Topics:** MDP formalism, finite-horizon planning, bit-flip dynamics, optimal policy / value, ramp-then-stay | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_02, lecture_03
**Statement (English translation):**
An MDP defined on the $n$-dimensional Boolean cube: $2^n$ states, the bit-strings in $\{0,1\}^n$; two nodes are adjacent iff they differ in exactly one bit. From each state there are $n+1$ deterministic actions $\{a_0,a_1,\dots,a_{n-1},a_*\}$: $a_*$ leaves the state unchanged, and $a_i$ flips bit $i$ (from 0 to 1 or from 1 to 0). For example, at state $0101$ ($n=4$): $a_*$ stays $0101$; $a_0\to1101$; $a_1\to0001$; $a_2\to0111$; $a_3\to0100$. Assume $n=2k$ (even); the initial state is $0^k1^k$, and we solve a **finite-horizon** problem of length $n=2k$. For a state $s=(s_1,\dots,s_n)$ let $n(s)=\sum_{i=1}^n s_i$ (the number of 1-bits); the reward is $r(s,a)=|n(s)-k|$ for every action $a$.

a. Write the MDP formally.

b. Write an optimal policy. (Note: you must define it for every state.)

c. What is the value function of the optimal policy? (Also defined for every state.)

d. Let $\pi^*$ be the optimal policy. Are there states whose optimal action is $a_*$ (i.e. $\pi^*(s)=a_*$)? If more than one, name all of them.

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** $S=\{0,1\}^n$, $A=\{a_0,\dots,a_{n-1},a_*\}$, $s_0=0^k1^k$, horizon $T=n=2k$. Deterministic transitions $P$: $a_*$ is the self-loop $s\to s$ and $a_i$ is $s\to s\oplus e_i$ (flip bit $i$). Reward $r(s,a)=|n(s)-k|$ depends only on the current state (independent of the action).

**(b)** Since the reward grows as $n(s)$ moves **away** from $k$ (peaking at $r=k$ when $n(s)\in\{0,2k\}$, i.e. all-zeros or all-ones), and the action affects only the next state, the optimal move is: **if $s$ is all-zeros or all-ones, play $a_*$ (stay); otherwise flip one bit to move $n(s)$ toward the nearer extreme** â€” flip a $1\to0$ when $n(s)\le k$, or a $0\to1$ when $n(s)\ge k$ (either extreme when $n(s)=k$). This "ramp to the nearest corner, then park there" reaches the maximum per-step reward $k$ as fast as possible and holds it.

**(c)** Let $d=\min\{n(s),\,2k-n(s)\}$ be the number of flips to the nearest extreme (so the current reward is $|n(s)-k|=k-d$). Ramping raises the reward $k-d,k-d+1,\dots,k$ over $d$ steps and then stays at $k$:
$$\begin{aligned}V^*(s)&=\sum_{j=0}^{d}\big(k-d+j\big)\ +\ (2k-1-d)\,k,\qquad\\ d&=\min\{n(s),2k-n(s)\}.\end{aligned}$$
In particular, from the initial state $n(s_0)=k$ so $d=k$ and

$$V^*(s_0)=\tfrac{k(k+1)}{2}+(k-1)k=\dfrac{k(3k-1)}{2}$$

at an extreme ($d=0$) $V^*=2k^2$. (Verified by exact finite-horizon DP for $k=1,2,3$: $V^*(s_0)=1,5,12=\tfrac{k(3k-1)}2$.)

**(d)** **Yes** â€” exactly the two all-equal states $\boxed{0^n\text{ and }1^n}$ (all-zeros and all-ones). There the reward already sits at its maximum $k$, so any flip would strictly lower the future reward; staying with $a_*$ is optimal. At every non-extreme state a flip toward an extreme raises the next reward, so $a_*$ is not optimal there. (Confirmed by DP: $a_*$ optimal precisely at $0^n,1^n$ for $k=1,2,3$.)

**đź’ˇ Trick.** The action never changes the *current* reward $|n(s)-k|$ â€” only the *next* state â€” so the whole problem is "steer $n(s)$ to a corner and park." Reward is maximal ($=k$) exactly at the two corners $0^n,1^n$, giving the ramp-then-stay optimum and the two $a_*$ states.

**âš ď¸Ź Watch out.** The reward is $|n(s)-k|$, a $V$-shape minimized at the balanced count $n(s)=k$ (where the start sits, reward $0$) and maximized at *both* extremes â€” don't mistake $n(s)=k$ for the goal; it is the worst state. And $a_*$ is optimal only at the corners, not wherever the reward is momentarily high.

## Q2 (30 pts) â€” Mixing two transition functions
**Topics:** MDP definition, transition mixture, nonlinearity of value in the transitions, optimal policy, convex combination | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_03, lecture_04
**Statement (English translation):**
Given a state set $S$, actions $A$, initial state $s_0\in S$ and reward $r$. For a transition function $p$ let $M_p=(S,A,p,s_0,r)$; let $V^\pi_p(s)$ be the value of $\pi$ from $s$ in $M_p$ and $V^*_p(s)$ the optimal value from $s$ in $M_p$. All rewards are in $[0,1]$ and the criterion is discounted reward with $\gamma=0.9$. Two different transition functions $p_1,p_2$ are given, and
$$p_3(s'\mid s,a)=0.5\,p_1(s'\mid s,a)+0.5\,p_2(s'\mid s,a)\quad\text{for all }s,s',a.$$

a. Is $p_3$ necessarily a legal transition function, and is $M_{p_3}$ necessarily a legal MDP? (Is it valid for all choices of $p_1,p_2$?)

b. Given a policy $\pi$. Is it necessarily true that $0.5\,V^\pi_{M_1}(s_0)+0.5\,V^\pi_{M_2}(s_0)=V^\pi_{M_3}(s_0)$?

c. Let $\pi_1$ be optimal for $M_{p_1}$ and $\pi_2$ optimal for $M_{p_2}$. Define $\pi_3(a\mid s)=0.5\,\pi_1(a\mid s)+0.5\,\pi_2(a\mid s)$. Is $\pi_3$ necessarily optimal for $M_{p_3}$?

d. Is it necessarily true that $0.5\,V^*_{M_1}(s_0)+0.5\,V^*_{M_2}(s_0)=V^*_{M_3}(s_0)$?

**Solution sketch:**
**(a) Yes.** For each $(s,a)$, $p_3(\cdot\mid s,a)$ is a **convex combination** of two probability distributions, hence a probability distribution: $p_3\ge0$ and $\sum_{s'}p_3(s'\mid s,a)=0.5\cdot1+0.5\cdot1=1$. So $p_3$ is legal and $M_{p_3}$ is a legal MDP, for every $p_1,p_2$.

**(b) No.** For a *fixed* policy, $V^\pi_p=(I-\gamma P^\pi_p)^{-1}r^\pi$ is a **nonlinear** (matrix-inverse) function of the transitions $P$, so averaging transitions is **not** averaging values. Counterexample: on random $2$â€“$3$ state MDPs with $\gamma=0.9$, $0.5V^\pi_{M_1}(s_0)+0.5V^\pi_{M_2}(s_0)\ne V^\pi_{M_3}(s_0)$ generically. (Confirmed numerically.)

**(c) No.** Mixing the two optimal *policies* need not be optimal under the mixed *transitions*: $\pi_3$ evaluated on $M_{p_3}$ can be strictly worse than $V^*_{M_3}$. Counterexample found on random MDPs where $V^{\pi_3}_{M_3}(s_0)<V^*_{M_3}(s_0)$. (Confirmed numerically.)

**(d) No.** The optimal value is likewise **not** linear in the transitions (it is a max over policies of the nonlinear $V^\pi_p$), so $0.5V^*_{M_1}(s_0)+0.5V^*_{M_2}(s_0)\ne V^*_{M_3}(s_0)$ in general. (Confirmed numerically on random MDPs.)

**đź’ˇ Trick.** Separate what is linear in the mixture from what is not. Transition **normalization** is linear, so a convex combination of transition rows is always legal (a). But value â€” for a fixed policy *or* optimized â€” passes through $(I-\gamma P)^{-1}$, which is nonlinear in $P$; hence *every* value identity (b, d) and the policy-mixing claim (c) fails.

**âš ď¸Ź Watch out.** Do not import the reward-mixing intuition here. $V^\pi$ is **linear in the reward** $r$ but **not** in the transition $p$ â€” mixing rewards averages values, mixing transitions does not. That single distinction answers (b) and (d) "no" at once.

## Q3 (20 pts) â€” Value approximation with a squared-linear model (MC-SGD)
**Topics:** value-function approximation, squared-linear model, gradient descent, Monte-Carlo return, stochastic gradient | **Pillar:** Approximation | **Difficulty:** 3
**Maps to:** lecture_08, lecture_06
**Statement (English translation):**
Given an episodic MDP where every state $s$ has a feature vector $\phi(s)\in\mathbb{R}^d$. A value-function approximator with parameter $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is $\hat V(s;\theta)=\big(\phi(s)\cdot\theta\big)^2$. For a policy $\pi$ we minimize
$$J^\pi(\theta)=\frac12\sum_s\mu(s)\big(V^\pi(s)-\hat V(s;\theta)\big)^2,$$
where $\mu(s)$ is the probability that policy $\pi$ reaches state $s$ (assume a state is not visited twice in the same run). We minimize by gradient updates.

a. Write the gradient-descent update for minimizing $J^\pi(\theta)$ (as a function of $\phi(s),\mu(s),V^\pi(s)$ and learning rate $\alpha$).

b. Propose an unbiased Monte-Carlo estimate of $V^\pi(s)$ from an episode of $\pi$ in which $s$ appears.

c. Write the Monte-Carlo SGD update for minimizing $J^\pi(\theta)$.

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** Here $\nabla_\theta\hat V(s;\theta)=2\big(\phi(s)\cdot\theta\big)\phi(s)$, so $\nabla_\theta J^\pi=\sum_s\mu(s)\big(\hat V(s;\theta)-V^\pi(s)\big)\,2(\phi(s)\cdot\theta)\,\phi(s)$ and
$$\theta^{t+1}=\theta^{t}-\alpha\sum_s\mu(s)\big(\hat V(s;\theta^t)-V^\pi(s)\big)\,2\big(\phi(s)\cdot\theta^t\big)\,\phi(s).$$

**(b)** From a visit to $s$ at time $t_s$, use the **return** $G_s=\sum_{k\ge t_s}\gamma^{\,k-t_s}r_k$ (the discounted sum of rewards after $s$); it is unbiased, $\mathbb{E}[G_s]=V^\pi(s)$.

**(c)** Sample states on-policy (realizing the $\mu(s)$ weighting) and replace $V^\pi(s_t)$ by $G_t$:
$$\theta^{t+1}=\theta^{t}-\alpha\big(\hat V(s_t;\theta^t)-G_t\big)\,2\big(\phi(s_t)\cdot\theta^t\big)\,\phi(s_t)=\theta^{t}+2\alpha\big(G_t-\hat V(s_t;\theta^t)\big)\big(\phi(s_t)\cdot\theta^t\big)\,\phi(s_t).$$

**đź’ˇ Trick.** Same MC-SGD recipe as any value-approximation objective â€” only the model's own derivative changes. For the squared-linear head $\hat V=(\phi\cdot\theta)^2$ the chain rule gives the factor $2(\phi(s)\cdot\theta)$; sampling states on-policy supplies $\mu$, and the return $G_t$ supplies an unbiased $V^\pi(s_t)$.

**âš ď¸Ź Watch out.** Keep the inner factor $2(\phi(s)\cdot\theta)$ from differentiating the square â€” dropping it (treating $\hat V$ as linear) gives the wrong gradient. Note this factor can flip sign with $\phi(s)\cdot\theta$, and the model is non-convex in $\theta$ (a squared linear form), so SGD only finds a stationary point.

## Q4 (20 pts) â€” Contraction of a policy-evaluation Q-operator
**Topics:** policy-evaluation $Q$ operator, $\gamma$-contraction in sup-norm, fixed point, $Q^\pi$ | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 3
**Maps to:** lecture_04, lecture_06
**Statement (English translation):**
Given an MDP $M=(S,A,P,s_0,r)$, all actions available from every state. For a deterministic policy $\pi$ define the operator $H^\pi$ on $Q\in\mathbb{R}^{|S|\cdot|A|}$, for all $s\in S,\ a\in A$, with $r(s,a)\in[0,1]$:
$$(H^\pi Q)(s,a)=\gamma\,r(s,a)+\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}\big(Q(s',\pi(s'))\big).$$

a. Write the definition of when an operator $H$ is $\gamma$-contracting for the norm $\|\cdot\|_\infty$.

b. Prove that $H^\pi$ is $\gamma$-contracting for the norm $\|\cdot\|_\infty$.

c. Let $\bar Q$ be the fixed point of $H^\pi$, i.e. $H^\pi\bar Q=\bar Q$. Write $\bar Q(s,a)$ as a function of $\gamma$ and/or the value functions of the MDP $M$.

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** $H$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ if $\|HQ-HQ'\|_\infty\le\gamma\|Q-Q'\|_\infty$ for all $Q,Q'$.

**(b)** The reward term $\gamma\,r(s,a)$ does not depend on $Q$, so it cancels:
$$(H^\pi Q)(s,a)-(H^\pi Q')(s,a)=\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}\big(Q(s',\pi(s'))-Q'(s',\pi(s'))\big).$$
Taking absolute values and using $\big|Q(s',\pi(s'))-Q'(s',\pi(s'))\big|\le\|Q-Q'\|_\infty$ and that an expectation of something bounded by $\|Q-Q'\|_\infty$ is at most $\|Q-Q'\|_\infty$,
$$\big|(H^\pi Q)(s,a)-(H^\pi Q')(s,a)\big|\le\gamma\|Q-Q'\|_\infty.$$
Taking the max over $(s,a)$ gives $\|H^\pi Q-H^\pi Q'\|_\infty\le\gamma\|Q-Q'\|_\infty$. By Banach there is a unique fixed point $\bar Q$.

**(c)** The fixed point equation $\bar Q(s,a)=\gamma r(s,a)+\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}\bar Q(s',\pi(s'))$ is exactly the standard policy-evaluation equation with an extra global factor of $\gamma$ on the reward. Writing $Q^\pi(s,a)=r(s,a)+\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}Q^\pi(s',\pi(s'))$ for the usual stateâ€“action value of $\pi$ in $M$, we get
$$\boxed{\bar Q(s,a)=\gamma\,Q^\pi(s,a)}=\gamma\Big(r(s,a)+\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}V^\pi(s')\Big),$$
where $V^\pi(s')=Q^\pi(s',\pi(s'))$ is the value of $\pi$. (Verified numerically: iterating $H^\pi$ converges to $\gamma\,Q^\pi$ on random MDPs.)

**đź’ˇ Trick.** The $\gamma r$ term is a constant in $Q$, so it cancels in the contraction â€” that is why $H^\pi$ is a plain $\gamma$-contraction, exactly like ordinary policy evaluation. For the fixed point, note $H^\pi$ *is* the policy-evaluation operator with $\gamma\cdot r$ in place of $r$; since $Q^\pi$ is linear in the reward, scaling $r$ by $\gamma$ scales the fixed point by $\gamma$: $\bar Q=\gamma Q^\pi$.

**âš ď¸Ź Watch out.** The look-ahead uses the **policy** action $Q(s',\pi(s'))$ (this is *policy evaluation*, a $\gamma$-contraction), not $\max_{a'}Q(s',a')$ (which would be Bellman *optimality*). And the extra $\gamma$ on $r$ matters: the fixed point is $\gamma Q^\pi$, not $Q^\pi$ â€” don't drop it.
