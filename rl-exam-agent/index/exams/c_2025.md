## Q1 (30 pts) — Selling to random offers: an optimal-stopping / inventory MDP
**Topics:** MDP formalism, finite-horizon optimal stopping, threshold policy, monotonicity in inventory, inventory management | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_03, lecture_04
**Statement (English translation):**
A sequential decision problem with parameters $k\ge1$, $m\ge1$, $T\ge1$. A seller has $k$ identical products. At each time $1\le t\le T$ a customer offers a price $p_t$ drawn uniformly from $[m]=\{1,\dots,m\}$, i.e. $\Pr[p_t=i]=1/m$ for $i\in[m]$. If the seller still has products, he responds to $p_t$ with one of two actions:
- **sell**: sell one of the remaining products, earn $p_t$, and remain with one fewer product.
- **hold**: do not sell, earn $0$, remain with the same number of products.

If the seller has no products left, the reward is $0$ for the remaining steps. The objective is the finite-horizon return with parameter $T\ge1$.

a. Write the MDP formally. (The initial state $s_0$ may be a distribution over states.)

b. Is it necessarily true that increasing the number of products $k$ increases the optimal expected profit? (I.e., for $k_1>k_2$, must 

$$\mathbb{E}_{s_0^1}V^*_{k_1,m,T}(s_0^1)>\mathbb{E}_{s_0^2}V^*_{k_2,m,T}(s_0^2)$$

?)

c. Given $p>q\ge1$ and $t,r\ge1$. Is it possible that the optimal policy at time $t$ with $r$ products left plays **sell** for $p_t=p$ and **hold** for $p_t=q$? Hint: check the optimality conditions on $Q^\pi$ for the proposed policy $\pi$.

d. Here $k=1$ and $m=2$. For each of the four options below, decide whether there are values of $T$ for which it is the optimal policy, and if so specify them. Option 1: the optimal policy always plays **sell** (every $t$, every $p_t$). Option 2: the optimal policy at $t=1$ plays **hold** for $p_1=1$ and **sell** for $p_1=2$. Option 3: the optimal policy at $t=2$ plays **sell** for $p_2=1$ and **hold** for $p_2=2$. Option 4: the optimal policy always plays **hold**.

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** State $s=(j,p)$ = (products left $j\in\{0,\dots,k\}$, current offered price $p\in[m]$); $A=\{\text{sell},\text{hold}\}$; horizon $T$. The initial state $s_0=(k,p_1)$ has $p_1\sim\text{Uniform}[m]$, so $s_0$ is a **distribution** over $\{(k,1),\dots,(k,m)\}$. Transitions from $(j,p)$ with $j\ge1$: **sell** earns $p$ and goes to $(j-1,p')$; **hold** earns $0$ and goes to $(j,p')$; in both, the next price $p'\sim\text{Uniform}[m]$. If $j=0$ the reward is $0$ (absorbing "sold out").

**(b) No.** More inventory is never worse, so $V^*$ is **non-decreasing** in $k$ — but not **strictly**: you can sell at most one product per step, so with $T$ steps you can sell at most $T$ times. Once $k\ge T$ the extra products are useless and the optimal profit **plateaus**: for $k_1>k_2\ge T$, $\mathbb{E}V^*_{k_1,m,T}=\mathbb{E}V^*_{k_2,m,T}$. Counterexample to strict growth: $k_1=T+1>k_2=T$ give equal optimal profit. (Confirmed by DP: $V^*_T(k)$ non-decreasing in $k$ with a plateau for $k\ge T$.)

**(c) Yes.** The optimal policy is a **threshold** policy: sell iff $p_t\ge$ (the continuation value of keeping the product), which does not depend on $p_t$. Since $p>q$, any threshold $\tau$ with $q<\tau\le p$ yields exactly "sell at $p$, hold at $q$." So it is not only possible but the typical shape. (Confirmed by DP: there exist $(t,r)$ where the optimal policy sells the high price and holds the low one.)

**(d)** With $k=1,m=2$ the continuation value $V_{\tau}$ (with $\tau$ steps remaining) is $V_1=1.5$ and 

$$V_\tau=\tfrac12\max(1,V_{\tau-1})+\tfrac12\max(2,V_{\tau-1})\in[1.5,2)$$

 for $\tau\ge1$; the last step sells any price ($V_0=0$), and any earlier step sells only $p=2$ (threshold $V_{\tau-1}\in(1.5,2)$). Hence:
- **Option 1 (always sell): only $T=1$** — with a single step you must sell at both prices; for $T\ge2$ you hold $p_1=1$.
- **Option 2 ($t=1$: hold $1$, sell $2$): all $T\ge2$** — at $t=1$ with $\ge2$ steps left the threshold exceeds $1$, so hold $1$, sell $2$.
- **Option 3 ($t=2$: sell $1$, hold $2$): no $T$** — this is "sell low, hold high," the reverse of a threshold policy, never optimal.
- **Option 4 (always hold): no $T$** — never selling earns $0$, always beaten by selling.

(Confirmed by DP over $T=1,\dots,6$: Option 1 optimal only at $T=1$, Option 2 at every $T\ge2$, Options 3,4 never.)

**💡 Trick.** The optimal rule is a price **threshold** (sell iff the offer beats the value of waiting), which immediately gives (c) — "sell high, hold low" is exactly a threshold — and (d) — early steps hold the low price, the last step sells anything. And you can sell at most one item per step, so profit saturates once $k\ge T$, answering (b).

**⚠️ Watch out.** The threshold only ever says "sell high, hold low," never the reverse, so Option 3 (sell $1$, hold $2$) is impossible — don't be tempted to fit a $T$ to it. And "more products" helps only up to $k=T$; beyond that the answer to (b) is a firm "no, not strictly."

## Q2 (30 pts) — Identifying the k-th order statistic of the arm means
**Topics:** stochastic multi-armed bandits, order-statistic / best-arm identification, Hoeffding + union bound, explore-then-commit, confidence intervals | **Pillar:** Bandits | **Difficulty:** 5
**Maps to:** lecture_10
**Statement (English translation):**
A MAB with $n$ arms; arm $a_i$ has a Bernoulli reward with parameter $p_i$, and there is a gap $\Delta>0$ with $|p_i-p_j|\ge\Delta$ for all $i\ne j$. For a set $S\subset[0,1]^n$, the **k-statistic** is the $k$-th smallest value: sorting $x_1\le\cdots\le x_n$, it is $x_k$. Let $p_k$ be the k-statistic of $S=\{p_i:i\in[n]\}$. An explore-exploit algorithm with parameter $m$: **explore** — sample each arm $i$ exactly $m$ times; **exploit** — compute each empirical mean $\hat p_i$, form $\hat S=\{\hat p_i\}$, take its k-statistic $\hat p_k$, and return the matching index $\hat\imath_k$.

a. Find $m>0$ as a function of $n,\delta,\varepsilon$ such that with probability $\ge1-\delta$, $|\hat p_i-p_i|\le\varepsilon/2$ for every arm $i$ (as small as possible up to the multiplicative constant).

b. Define $S_0=\{i:p_i\le p_k-\varepsilon\}$, $S_1=\{i:p_i\ge p_k+\varepsilon\}$, $T_0=\{i:p_i\le p_k\}$, $T_1=\{i:p_i\ge p_k\}$. Assuming (from part a) $|\hat p_i-p_i|\le\varepsilon/2$ for all $i$, prove: (1) for $i\in S_1$ and $j\in T_0$, $\hat p_i\ge\hat p_j$; (2) for $i\in S_0$ and $j\in T_1$, $\hat p_i\le\hat p_j$.

c. Prove that $\hat\imath_k\notin S_1\cup S_0$. Hint: use part (b).

d. Show that $|p_{\hat\imath_k}-p_k|\le\varepsilon$.

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** For one arm, $m$ Bernoulli samples give 

$$\Pr[|\hat p_i-p_i|>\varepsilon/2]\le2e^{-2m(\varepsilon/2)^2}=2e^{-m\varepsilon^2/2}$$

 (Hoeffding). Union-bounding over the $n$ arms and forcing the total to $\delta$: $2n\,e^{-m\varepsilon^2/2}\le\delta$, i.e.
$$m=\Big\lceil\frac{2}{\varepsilon^2}\ln\!\frac{2n}{\delta}\Big\rceil.$$
(Numerically checked: this $m$ gives union failure $\le\delta$.)

**(b)** Both use $|\hat p-p|\le\varepsilon/2$. **(1)** $i\in S_1\Rightarrow p_i\ge p_k+\varepsilon$, so 

$$\hat p_i\ge p_i-\tfrac\varepsilon2\ge p_k+\tfrac\varepsilon2$$

; $j\in T_0\Rightarrow p_j\le p_k$, so 

$$\hat p_j\le p_j+\tfrac\varepsilon2\le p_k+\tfrac\varepsilon2$$

. Hence $\hat p_i\ge p_k+\tfrac\varepsilon2\ge\hat p_j$. **(2)** symmetric: $i\in S_0\Rightarrow\hat p_i\le p_k-\tfrac\varepsilon2$ and $j\in T_1\Rightarrow\hat p_j\ge p_k-\tfrac\varepsilon2$, so $\hat p_i\le\hat p_j$.

**(c)** $\hat p_k$ (the returned k-statistic of $\hat S$) is the $k$-th smallest empirical mean. Suppose $\hat\imath_k\in S_1$. Since $p_k$ is the $k$-th smallest true value, $|T_0|\ge k$, and $\hat\imath_k\notin T_0$; by b(1), $\hat p_{\hat\imath_k}\ge\hat p_j$ for all $j\in T_0$, so at least $k$ other arms have empirical mean $\le\hat p_{\hat\imath_k}$, making its rank $\ge k+1$ — contradicting rank $k$. Suppose $\hat\imath_k\in S_0$. Then $|T_1|\ge n-k+1$, $\hat\imath_k\notin T_1$; by b(2), $\hat p_{\hat\imath_k}\le\hat p_j$ for all $j\in T_1$, so at most $k-1$ arms lie strictly below it, making its rank $\le k-1$ — again contradicting rank $k$. Therefore $\hat\imath_k\notin S_0\cup S_1$.

**(d)** $\hat\imath_k\notin S_0\cup S_1$ means neither $p_{\hat\imath_k}\le p_k-\varepsilon$ nor $p_{\hat\imath_k}\ge p_k+\varepsilon$, i.e.

$$p_k-\varepsilon<p_{\hat\imath_k}<p_k+\varepsilon$$

so $|p_{\hat\imath_k}-p_k|\le\varepsilon$. The algorithm returns an arm whose true mean is within $\varepsilon$ of the k-statistic.

**💡 Trick.** The whole proof is "estimate everything to $\pm\varepsilon/2$, then a rank argument." An $\varepsilon/2$ confidence radius (Hoeffding + union over $n$ arms) keeps every empirical mean on the correct side of $p_k\pm\varepsilon/2$; then any arm far from $p_k$ (in $S_0$ or $S_1$) is pushed past $\ge k$ or $\ge n-k+1$ competitors, so its empirical rank can't be exactly $k$.

**⚠️ Watch out.** Match the radius to the margin: with buffers $S_{0/1}$ at $\pm\varepsilon$ you need each estimate accurate to $\varepsilon/2$ (not $\varepsilon$) so the $T$-sets and $S$-sets separate. And the union bound is over **all $n$ arms** (the $\ln(2n/\delta)$ factor) — using a single-arm Hoeffding without the $n$ gives the wrong confidence.

## Q3 (20 pts) — Value approximation with a bilinear model (MC-SGD)
**Topics:** value-function approximation, bilinear/linear-in-$\theta$ model, gradient descent, Monte-Carlo return, stochastic gradient | **Pillar:** Approximation | **Difficulty:** 3
**Maps to:** lecture_08, lecture_06
**Statement (English translation):**
Given an MDP where every state $s$ has a feature vector $\phi(s)\in\mathbb{R}^d$. A value-function approximator with parameter $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is $\hat V(s;\theta)=\theta^\top M\phi(s)$, where $M\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ is a known fixed matrix. For a policy $\pi$ we minimize
$$J^\pi(\theta)=\frac12\sum_s\mu(s)\big(V^\pi(s)-\hat V(s;\theta)\big)^2,$$
where $\mu(s)$ is the probability that policy $\pi$ reaches state $s$ (assume a state is not visited twice in the same run). We minimize by gradient updates.

a. Write the gradient-descent update for minimizing $J^\pi(\theta)$ (as a function of $\phi(s),\mu(s),M,V^\pi(s)$ and learning rate $\alpha$).

b. Propose an unbiased Monte-Carlo estimate of $V^\pi(s)$ from an episode of $\pi$ in which $s$ appears.

c. Write the Monte-Carlo SGD update for minimizing $J^\pi(\theta)$.

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** The model is **linear** in $\theta$: $\hat V(s;\theta)=\theta^\top M\phi(s)$ so $\nabla_\theta\hat V(s;\theta)=M\phi(s)$. Hence 

$$\nabla_\theta J^\pi=\sum_s\mu(s)\big(\hat V(s;\theta)-V^\pi(s)\big)M\phi(s)$$

 and
$$\theta^{t+1}=\theta^{t}-\alpha\sum_s\mu(s)\big(\hat V(s;\theta^t)-V^\pi(s)\big)\,M\phi(s).$$

**(b)** From a visit to $s$ at time $t_s$, use the **return** $G_s=\sum_{k\ge t_s}\gamma^{\,k-t_s}r_k$; it is unbiased, $\mathbb{E}[G_s]=V^\pi(s)$.

**(c)** Sample states on-policy (realizing the $\mu(s)$ weighting) and replace $V^\pi(s_t)$ by $G_t$:
$$\theta^{t+1}=\theta^{t}-\alpha\big(\hat V(s_t;\theta^t)-G_t\big)M\phi(s_t)=\theta^{t}+\alpha\big(G_t-\theta^{t\top}M\phi(s_t)\big)M\phi(s_t).$$

**💡 Trick.** Same MC-SGD template; only the model derivative changes. Because $\hat V=\theta^\top M\phi(s)$ is **linear** in $\theta$, its gradient is simply the fixed vector $M\phi(s)$ (a re-featurization of $\phi$ by $M$). Sampling states on-policy supplies $\mu$; the return $G_t$ supplies an unbiased $V^\pi(s_t)$.

**⚠️ Watch out.** Since $M$ is **fixed/known** (not a parameter), you differentiate only w.r.t. $\theta$: the gradient is $M\phi(s)$, not anything involving $\nabla M$. Treat $M\phi(s)$ as the effective feature; the objective is convex quadratic in $\theta$ (unlike a squared or exponential head).

## Q4 (20 pts) — Contraction of a min–min operator (and its trivial fixed point)
**Topics:** Bellman-type $Q$ operator, $\gamma$-contraction in sup-norm, fixed point, non-negativity of rewards | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 3
**Maps to:** lecture_04, lecture_03
**Statement (English translation):**
Given an MDP $M=(S,A,p,s_0,r)$ with $r(s,a)\in[0,1]$ for all $s,a$. Define the operator $T_M$ for $U\in\mathbb{R}^{|S\times A|}$, with $0<\gamma<1$:
$$(T_MU)(s,a)=\min\Big\{r(s,a),\ \gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}\big[\min_{a'}U(s',a')\big]\Big\}.$$

a. Write the definition of when an operator $H$ is $\gamma$-contracting for the norm $\|\cdot\|_\infty$.

b. Prove that $T_M$ is $\gamma$-contracting for the norm $\|\cdot\|_\infty$. Hint: it can be proved for each $(s,a)$ separately, splitting into cases by which term attains the min.

c. Let $U^*$ be the fixed point of $T_M$. What is $U^*(s,a)$?

**Solution sketch:**
**(a)** $H$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ if $\|HU-HU'\|_\infty\le\gamma\|U-U'\|_\infty$ for all $U,U'$.

**(b)** Use two non-expansiveness facts. First, "min with a fixed number" is non-expansive: $\big|\min\{c,x\}-\min\{c,y\}\big|\le|x-y|$ for any constant $c$ (check the three branches). Apply it per $(s,a)$ with $c=r(s,a)$, $x=\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'}\min_{a'}U(s',a')$, $y=\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'}\min_{a'}U'(s',a')$:
$$\big|(T_MU)(s,a)-(T_MU')(s,a)\big|\le|x-y|=\gamma\Big|\mathbb{E}_{s'}\big[\min_{a'}U(s',a')-\min_{a'}U'(s',a')\big]\Big|.$$
Second, 

$$\big|\min_{a'}U(s',a')-\min_{a'}U'(s',a')\big|\le\max_{a'}|U(s',a')-U'(s',a')|\le\|U-U'\|_\infty$$

, and the expectation of something bounded by $\|U-U'\|_\infty$ is at most $\|U-U'\|_\infty$. Hence each coordinate is at most $\gamma\|U-U'\|_\infty$, so $\|T_MU-T_MU'\|_\infty\le\gamma\|U-U'\|_\infty$. By Banach there is a unique fixed point $U^*$.

**(c)** $\boxed{U^*(s,a)=0\ \text{for all }(s,a)}$. Check that $U\equiv0$ is a fixed point: $\min_{a'}0=0$, so $\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'}[0]=0$, and since $r(s,a)\ge0$, $\min\{r(s,a),0\}=0$. Thus $T_M(0)=0$, and by the uniqueness from (b) this is *the* fixed point. (Verified numerically: value iteration from random $U$ converges to $0$ on random MDPs.)

**💡 Trick.** Non-negativity does everything. Because $r\ge0$ and the look-ahead is a **min** shrunk by $\gamma$, the operator can never push a value above $0$ from below or hold it above $0$ — the discounted min drags the future term toward $0$ and $\min\{r,0\}=0$. So the unique fixed point is the all-zeros function.

**⚠️ Watch out.** Contrast with the **max** version (which yields "local reward $+$ a positive tail"): here every $\min$ and the $\gamma$-shrunk future collapse the fixed point to $0$. Don't look for a reward-dependent formula — the rewards' non-negativity is exactly why $U^*\equiv0$. The contraction proof is otherwise identical to the max case (the $|\min\{c,x\}-\min\{c,y\}|\le|x-y|$ lemma replaces its max twin).
