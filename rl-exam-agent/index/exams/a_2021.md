## Q1 (30 pts) — Queue admission control: MDP and threshold optimal policy
**Topics:** MDP formalization, policy evaluation, structure of the optimal policy, threshold policy, monotonicity | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_03, lecture_04
**Statement (English translation):**
A finite queue can hold up to $H$ units. At each time $t$:

(a) a unit arrives with value $v$ sampled from the uniform distribution over $\{0,\dots,N\}$;

(b) if the number of units currently in the queue is less than $H$ we decide whether to **add** the unit to the queue (gaining reward $v$) or **not add** it (gaining $0$); otherwise (the queue already holds $H$ units) the unit is not added;

(c) with probability $q$ the queue empties by one unit (one unit departs).
At $t=0$ the queue holds a single unit whose value is $v\sim\text{Uniform}\{0,\dots,N\}$. The return is discounted with parameter $\gamma>0$ ($\gamma<1$).

- **(a)** Write the MDP of the problem formally: give $S,\ A,\ s_0,\ R,\ P$.
- **(b)** What is the value function $V^\pi$ when the policy $\pi$ **always accepts whenever possible**?
- **(c1)** Let $\pi^*$ be the optimal policy (for the discounted return with $\gamma>0$). Suppose that at time $t$ the optimal policy accepts a unit of value $v$. Can we conclude that in the same state the optimal policy accepts **every** unit of value $v'>v$? (yes/no, explain.)
- **(c2)** Suppose that at time $t$ the optimal policy does **not** accept a unit of value $v$. Can we conclude that in the same state the optimal policy will not accept every unit of value $v'<v$? (yes/no, explain.)
- **(d)** Show that the optimal policy can be characterized by a sequence of thresholds $\theta_0,\dots,\theta_H$ such that a unit of value $v$ is accepted, when the queue holds $k$ units, if and only if $v\ge\theta_k$. (Hint: use part (c).)

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** State encodes the current occupancy and the value of the arriving unit: $s=(m,v)$ with $m\in\{0,\dots,H\}$ units in queue and $v\in\{0,\dots,N\}$ the arriving value; $S=\{0,\dots,H\}\times\{0,\dots,N\}$. Actions $A=\{\text{accept},\text{reject}\}$, with accept feasible only when $m<H$ (when $m=H$ the only outcome is reject). $s_0=(1,v),\ v\sim\text{Uniform}\{0,\dots,N\}$. Reward $R((m,v),\text{accept})=v$ (for $m<H$), $R((m,v),\text{reject})=0$. Dynamics: after the decision the count becomes $m'$ ($m{+}1$ if accepted, $m$ otherwise). Then with prob. $q$ one unit departs ($m'\to\max(m'-1,0)$) and with prob. $1-q$ it stays. Finally a fresh $v'\sim\text{Uniform}\{0,\dots,N\}$ is drawn independently. So $P\big((m,v)\!\to\!(m'',v')\big)=\tfrac{1}{N+1}\big[q\,\mathbb 1\{m''=m'-1\}+(1-q)\mathbb 1\{m''=m'\}\big]$.

**(b)** Policy evaluation (Bellman) for "always accept when possible": for $m<H$, $V^\pi(m,v)=v+\gamma\,\mathbb E_{v'}\!\big[q\,V^\pi(m,v')+(1-q)V^\pi(m{+}1,v')\big]$; for $m=H$, $V^\pi(H,v)=\gamma\,\mathbb E_{v'}\!\big[q\,V^\pi(H{-}1,v')+(1-q)V^\pi(H,v')\big]$. The continuation term depends on $m$ only, so $V^\pi(m,v)=v\cdot\mathbb 1\{m<H\}+c_m$ for constants $c_0,\dots,c_H$ obtained by solving this $(H{+}1)$-variable linear system (with $\mathbb E[v']=N/2$). Note "always accept" is generally **not** optimal — it wastes scarce capacity on low-value units.

**(c1)** **Yes.** The advantage of accepting over rejecting in state $(m,v)$ is

$$\begin{aligned}A(m,v)&=Q^*((m,v),\text{acc})-Q^*((m,v),\text{rej})\\ &=v-\theta_m\end{aligned}$$

where $\theta_m=\gamma\big(\mathbb E[V^*\mid m\text{ units continue}]-\mathbb E[V^*\mid m{+}1\text{ units}]\big)$ does **not** depend on $v$ (the value $v$ affects only the immediate reward and the resulting count, not the distribution of the next arrival). Since $A$ is increasing in $v$, accepting at $v$ ($A\ge0$) implies accepting at all $v'>v$.

**(c2)** **Yes**, same monotonicity: rejecting at $v$ ($A(m,v)\le0$) implies $A(m,v')\le0$ for all $v'<v$, so those are rejected too.

**(d)** By (c) the accept-vs-reject advantage at occupancy $k$ equals $v-\theta_k$ with $\theta_k$ a constant independent of $v$. The optimal policy accepts iff $v-\theta_k\ge0$, i.e. iff $v\ge\theta_k$; a full queue ($k=H$) never accepts ($\theta_H=+\infty$). This is exactly the claimed threshold characterization $\{\theta_0,\dots,\theta_H\}$.

**💡 Trick.** Write the accept-vs-reject advantage as $A(m,v)=v-\theta_m$ where $\theta_m$ depends on the occupancy $m$ but not on $v$; monotonicity in $v$ then hands you both parts (c) and the threshold policy in (d) at once.

**⚠️ Watch out.** Don't claim "always accept" is optimal — it wastes scarce capacity on low-value units — and remember the full queue $k=H$ never accepts, i.e. $\theta_H=+\infty$.

## Q2 (30 pts) — Bernoulli MAB with the (pessimistic) LCB algorithm
**Topics:** multi-armed bandits, Hoeffding confidence intervals, UCB/LCB, exploration failure, regret | **Pillar:** Bandits | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_10
**Statement (English translation):**
Consider a MAB with $k$ arms. The reward of arm $a_i$ is Bernoulli with parameter $q_i$ (equals $1$ w.p. $q_i$, else $0$). Define $i^*=\arg\max_i q_i$ and $q^*=\max_i q_i$; all $q_i$ are unknown. Define the **LCB algorithm**: for $t\le k$ play $a_t=t$ (pull each arm once); for $t>k$ play $a_t=\arg\max_i \mathrm{LCB}^t_i$ and receive reward $r_t$. Let $n^t_i$ be the number of pulls of arm $i$ up to time $t$, and $\hat\mu^t_i=\frac{1}{n^t_i}\sum_{t:a_t=i} r_t$ its empirical mean.
- **(a)** Given a sample of $n^t_i$ rewards from arm $a_i$ of which $s^t_i$ are $1$, define $\mathrm{UCB}(n^t_i,s^t_i)$ and $\mathrm{LCB}(n^t_i,s^t_i)$ as functions of $\delta$ so that with probability $\ge 1-\delta$, $q_i\in[\mathrm{LCB},\mathrm{UCB}]$.
- From here on assume the UCB/LCB bounds always hold.
- **(b)** Compute a bound $M^*$ such that if arm $i^*$ has been pulled at least $M^*$ times then $a_t=i^*$ in the LCB algorithm.
- **(c)** Suppose $k=2$ with $q_1=\tfrac34,\ q_2=\tfrac13$, and that we observed $r_1=0$ (from pulling arm 1 at $t=1$) and $r_2=1$ (from arm 2 at $t=2$). What is the action $a_3$?
- **(d)** Is there a bound $M$ (a function of $q_1,\dots,q_k$ and $T$, intended $M\ll T$) such that every suboptimal arm ($a_i\ne i^*$) is played at most $M$ times? (yes/no, explain.)

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** By Hoeffding on $[0,1]$ rewards, with radius $r(n)=\sqrt{\ln(2/\delta)/(2n)}$:

$$\mathrm{UCB}=\frac{s^t_i}{n^t_i}+\sqrt{\frac{\ln(2/\delta)}{2n^t_i}}$$

and

$$\mathrm{LCB}=\frac{s^t_i}{n^t_i}-\sqrt{\frac{\ln(2/\delta)}{2n^t_i}}$$

then $\Pr[q_i\in[\mathrm{LCB},\mathrm{UCB}]]\ge1-\delta$.

**(b)** For $a_t=i^*$ we need $\mathrm{LCB}_{i^*}>\mathrm{LCB}_j$ for all $j\neq i^*$. Under the assumption the intervals hold: $\mathrm{LCB}_j\le q_j$, and $\mathrm{LCB}_{i^*}\ge q^*-2r(n_{i^*})$ (since $\hat\mu_{i^*}\ge q^*-r$). It suffices that $q^*-2r(n_{i^*})>\max_{j\ne i^*}q_j$, i.e. $2r(n_{i^*})<\Delta:=q^*-\max_{j\ne i^*}q_j$. Solving $2\sqrt{\ln(2/\delta)/(2n_{i^*})}<\Delta$ gives $n_{i^*}>\dfrac{2\ln(2/\delta)}{\Delta^2}$, so $\boxed{M^*=\dfrac{2\ln(2/\delta)}{\Delta^2}}$ (gap $\Delta=q^*-\max_{j\ne i^*}q_j$).

**(c)** After $t=2$, each arm pulled once ($n=1$), so both LCBs share the same radius; $\hat\mu_1=0<\hat\mu_2=1$, hence $\mathrm{LCB}_2>\mathrm{LCB}_1$ and $a_3=\arg\max\mathrm{LCB}=$ **arm 2** (python-verified: e.g. $\delta=0.1\Rightarrow\mathrm{LCB}_1=-1.224,\ \mathrm{LCB}_2=-0.224$). Note arm 2 is the **truly suboptimal** arm ($q_2<q_1$): a single unlucky draw of the best arm makes LCB pick the worse one.

**(d)** **No.** Part (c) is the counterexample: after the best arm returns $0$ once it is never pulled again (its $\mathrm{LCB}$ stays low), while the suboptimal arm keeps being pulled, its interval shrinks and its $\mathrm{LCB}\to q_2$, so it is chosen $\Theta(T)$ times. "$\arg\max$ LCB" is purely pessimistic/exploitative and never explores. A suboptimal arm can be played $\sim T$ times, so no bound $M\ll T$ exists (the premise of (b) — reaching $M^*$ pulls of $i^*$ — may never be met).

**💡 Trick.** Bound the LCB gap with the Hoeffding radius: forcing $2r(n_{i^*})<\Delta$ yields $M^*=2\ln(2/\delta)/\Delta^2$, and the same argmax-LCB rule that gives (b) is exactly what breaks in (d).

**⚠️ Watch out.** Argmax-LCB is purely exploitative — it never explores — so one unlucky $r=0$ from the best arm keeps its $\mathrm{LCB}$ low forever; don't assume the premise "$i^*$ reaches $M^*$ pulls" is ever met.

## Q3 (20 pts) — Value-function fitting (SGD / Monte-Carlo) and REINFORCE with a Laplace policy
**Topics:** function approximation, SGD, Monte-Carlo return estimation, policy gradient, REINFORCE, score function | **Pillar:** Approximation | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_08, lecture_09
**Statement (English translation):**
*Setup 1.* Every state $s$ has a feature vector $\phi(s)\in\mathbb R^d$. A value function is parameterized by $\theta_1,\theta_2\in\mathbb R^d$ as $V(s;\theta_1,\theta_2)=\tfrac12(\phi(s)\cdot\theta_2)^2+\phi(s)\cdot\theta_1$. For a policy $\pi$ we minimize the squared error $J^\pi(\theta_1,\theta_2)=\sum_s\mu(s)\big(V^\pi(s)-V(s;\theta_1,\theta_2)\big)^2$, where $\mu(s)$ is $\pi$'s stationary distribution (assume every state is visited many times). Minimize $J^\pi$ by gradient updates.
- **(a)** Write the gradient-descent update for $\theta_1^{t+1},\theta_2^{t+1}$ (as a function of $\phi(s),\mu(s),V^\pi(s)$ and learning rate $\alpha$).
- **(b)** Propose how to obtain an unbiased estimate of $V^\pi(s)$ for a state $s\sim\mu$ using Monte-Carlo.
- **(c)** Write the Monte-Carlo SGD update for minimizing $J^\pi$.

*Setup 2.* The Laplace distribution with parameter $\mu$ has density $\tfrac12\exp(-|x-\mu|)$ for all $x$. Actions $a\ge0$ in state $s$ are drawn from this Laplace distribution with parameter $\mu=\theta\cdot\phi(s)$, where policy $\pi$ is characterized by $\theta\in\mathbb R^d$.
- **(d)** What is the probability (density) that action $a\ge0$ is drawn in state $s$?
- **(e)** Compute $\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a\mid s;\theta)$.
- **(f)** Write the REINFORCE update for the Laplace distribution.

**Solution sketch:**
Gradients of the model: $\nabla_{\theta_1}V=\phi(s)$, $\nabla_{\theta_2}V=(\phi(s)\cdot\theta_2)\,\phi(s)$.

**(a)** (folding the factor $2$ into $\alpha$)
$\theta_1^{t+1}=\theta_1^{t}+\alpha\sum_s\mu(s)\big(V^\pi(s)-V(s;\theta)\big)\phi(s)$;
$\theta_2^{t+1}=\theta_2^{t}+\alpha\sum_s\mu(s)\big(V^\pi(s)-V(s;\theta)\big)(\phi(s)\cdot\theta_2^{t})\,\phi(s)$.

**(b)** From state $s$ roll out one episode under $\pi$ and take the discounted return $\hat G=\sum_{k\ge0}\gamma^k r_{t+k}$; then $\mathbb E[\hat G\mid s]=V^\pi(s)$, an unbiased estimate.

**(c)** Sample $s\sim\mu$ (a visited state), form the MC target $\hat G(s)$, and update
$\theta_1^{t+1}=\theta_1^{t}+\alpha(\hat G(s)-V(s;\theta))\phi(s)$, $\ \theta_2^{t+1}=\theta_2^{t}+\alpha(\hat G(s)-V(s;\theta))(\phi(s)\cdot\theta_2^{t})\phi(s)$.

**(d)**

$$\pi(a\mid s;\theta)=\tfrac12\exp\!\big(-|a-\theta\cdot\phi(s)|\big)$$

for $a\ge0$.

**(e)** $\log\pi=\log\tfrac12-|a-\theta\cdot\phi(s)|$, so

$$\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a\mid s;\theta)=\operatorname{sign}\!\big(a-\theta\cdot\phi(s)\big)\,\phi(s)$$

(i.e. $+\phi(s)$ if $a>\theta\cdot\phi(s)$, $-\phi(s)$ if $a<\theta\cdot\phi(s)$).

**(f)** REINFORCE:

$$\begin{aligned}\theta^{t+1}&=\theta^{t}+\alpha\,G_t\,\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a_t\mid s_t;\theta)\\ &=\theta^{t}+\alpha\,G_t\,\operatorname{sign}\!\big(a_t-\theta\cdot\phi(s_t)\big)\phi(s_t)\end{aligned}$$

where $G_t=\sum_{k\ge0}\gamma^k r_{t+k}$ is the return from time $t$ (optionally with a baseline / $\gamma^t$ weighting).

**💡 Trick.** Everything reduces to two chain-rule facts: $\nabla_{\theta_2}V=(\phi\cdot\theta_2)\phi$ for the quadratic model, and $\nabla_\theta\log\pi=\operatorname{sign}(a-\theta\cdot\phi)\,\phi$ for the Laplace score; plug an unbiased MC return $\hat G$ in for the unknown $V^\pi$ / $G_t$.

**⚠️ Watch out.** Mind the sign when differentiating $|a-\theta\cdot\phi(s)|$ — the two minus signs cancel to give $+\operatorname{sign}(a-\theta\cdot\phi)\phi$ — and use the sampled return, never the true $V^\pi(s)$, as the SGD target.

## Q4 (20 pts) — Log-transition Bellman operator: γ-contraction and its reward
**Topics:** Bellman operator, γ-contraction, sup-norm, fixed point, Bellman optimality equation | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 3
**Maps to:** lecture_04, lecture_03
**Statement (English translation):**
Given an MDP $M=(S,A,p,s_0,R)$, define for $U\in\mathbb R^{|S|}$ the operator
$$(TU)(s)=\max_a \mathbb E_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}\big[\ln p(s'\mid s,a)+\gamma U(s')\big].$$
- **(a)** State the definition of an operator $H$ being $\gamma$-contracting with respect to the norm $\lVert\cdot\rVert_\infty$.
- **(b)** Prove that $T$ is $\gamma$-contracting with respect to $\lVert\cdot\rVert_\infty$.
- **(c)** Define a reward function $r(s,a)$ so that $TV^{\pi^*}=V^{\pi^*}$, where $\pi^*$ is the optimal policy and $V^{\pi^*}$ is its discounted return with parameter $\gamma$. (Give $r(s,a)$ and explain.)

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** $H$ is $\gamma$-contracting in $\lVert\cdot\rVert_\infty$ iff for all $U,V$:

$$\lVert HU-HV\rVert_\infty\le\gamma\lVert U-V\rVert_\infty$$

**(b)** Fix $s$. Using $|\max_a f(a)-\max_a g(a)|\le\max_a|f(a)-g(a)|$ and that the $\ln p(s'\mid s,a)$ term is identical in $TU$ and $TV$:

$$|(TU)(s)-(TV)(s)|\le\max_a\big|\gamma\,\mathbb E_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}[U(s')-V(s')]\big|\le\gamma\max_a\mathbb E_{s'}|U(s')-V(s')|\le\gamma\lVert U-V\rVert_\infty.$$

Taking the max over $s$ gives $\lVert TU-TV\rVert_\infty\le\gamma\lVert U-V\rVert_\infty$. $\square$

**(c)** Take $r(s,a)=\mathbb E_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}[\ln p(s'\mid s,a)]=\sum_{s'}p(s'\mid s,a)\ln p(s'\mid s,a)=-\mathcal H\big(p(\cdot\mid s,a)\big)$ (negative entropy of the transition distribution, a function of $(s,a)$ only). Then $(TU)(s)=\max_a\big[r(s,a)+\gamma\,\mathbb E_{s'}U(s')\big]$ is exactly the Bellman **optimality** operator for the MDP with reward $r$. Its unique fixed point is $V^*=V^{\pi^*}$, hence $TV^{\pi^*}=V^{\pi^*}$.

**💡 Trick.** The $\ln p(s'\mid s,a)$ term is identical in $TU$ and $TV$, so it cancels in the contraction proof; reading it as the reward, $r(s,a)=\mathbb E_{s'}[\ln p]=-\mathcal H(p(\cdot\mid s,a))$ turns $T$ into a standard Bellman optimality operator.

**⚠️ Watch out.** The reward is $\mathbb E_{s'\sim p}[\ln p(s'\mid s,a)]$ (a function of $(s,a)$ only), not the raw $\ln p(s'\mid s,a)$; and use $|\max_a f-\max_a g|\le\max_a|f-g|$ to handle the $\max$ in the contraction step.
