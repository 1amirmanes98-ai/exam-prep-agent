## Q1 (30 pts) — Recycling-robot MDP: policy evaluation and optimal-action proofs
**Topics:** MDP formalism, policy evaluation, optimal policy, discounted return | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_02, lecture_03
**Statement (English translation):**
A vacuum-cleaning robot runs on a battery with three states: **high**, **low**, **empty**. Each step the robot chooses among 3 actions: **clean**, **wait**, **charge**. Dynamics:
- **charge**: battery → high, reward $0$.
- **wait**: battery stays in the same state, reward $R_{wait}$ (with $0<R_{wait}$).
- **clean**: reward $R_{clean}$ (with $R_{wait}<R_{clean}$), and the battery updates: from **high**, w.p. $1/3$ → low and w.p. $2/3$ stays high; from **low**, w.p. $1/2$ → empty and w.p. $1/2$ stays low.
- In state **empty** only **charge** is allowed: a human must recharge, reward $-10$, battery → high.

The battery starts in **high**. Criterion: discounted reward, $\gamma=0.9$.

(a) Write the MDP formally ($S,A,s_0,R,P$).

(b) The policy $\pi$ does **clean** in high and **charge** in low. Write $V^\pi(\text{high})$ and $V^\pi(\text{low})$ (as functions of $R_{clean}$).

(c) Prove that for all $0<R_{wait}<R_{clean}$ the optimal policy does **clean** in state high.

(d) For each pair $(R_{clean},R_{wait})$ with $0<R_{wait}<R_{clean}$, state which action the optimal policy does in **low** (clean / charge / wait), with a brief (informal) explanation: (i) $R_{clean}=1000,R_{wait}=1$; (ii) $R_{clean}=1,R_{wait}=0.99$; (iii) $R_{clean}=1,R_{wait}=0.01$.

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** $S=\{\text{high},\text{low},\text{empty}\}$, $A=\{\text{clean},\text{wait},\text{charge}\}$ (in empty only charge is allowed), $s_0=\text{high}$.

Rewards: $r(\cdot,\text{wait})=R_{wait}$, $r(\cdot,\text{clean})=R_{clean}$, $r(\{\text{high,low}\},\text{charge})=0$, $r(\text{empty},\text{charge})=-10$.

Transitions: charge → high w.p. 1; wait stays put; clean from high: $2/3$ high, $1/3$ low; clean from low: $1/2$ low, $1/2$ empty; empty+charge → high.

**(b)** Under this $\pi$ the robot never reaches empty, so its two value equations are

$$V^\pi(\text{high})=R_{clean}+\gamma\Big(\tfrac23 V^\pi(\text{high})+\tfrac13 V^\pi(\text{low})\Big),\qquad V^\pi(\text{low})=\gamma\,V^\pi(\text{high}).$$

Substituting the second into the first,

$$V^\pi(\text{high})\Big[1-\tfrac23\gamma-\tfrac13\gamma^2\Big]=R_{clean}.$$

With $\gamma=0.9$ we have $1-\tfrac23(0.9)-\tfrac13(0.81)=\tfrac{13}{100}$, hence

$$\boxed{\,V^\pi(\text{high})=\tfrac{100}{13}R_{clean}\approx 7.6923\,R_{clean}\,},\qquad \boxed{\,V^\pi(\text{low})=\tfrac{90}{13}R_{clean}\approx 6.9231\,R_{clean}\,}.$$

(Python-verified.)

**(c)** First, **charge** is dominated at high: since $R_{wait}>0$,

$$Q(\text{high,charge})=\gamma V^*(\text{high})<R_{wait}+\gamma V^*(\text{high})=Q(\text{high,wait}),$$

so the optimum at high is **wait** or **clean**. Comparing those two,

$$Q(\text{high,clean})-Q(\text{high,wait})=(R_{clean}-R_{wait})-\tfrac{\gamma}{3}\big(V^*(\text{high})-V^*(\text{low})\big).$$

Suppose, for contradiction, that **wait** were optimal at high; then $V^*(\text{high})=\tfrac{R_{wait}}{1-\gamma}$. But from low the robot can also wait forever, so 

$$V^*(\text{low})\ge\tfrac{R_{wait}}{1-\gamma}=V^*(\text{high})$$

, i.e. $V^*(\text{high})-V^*(\text{low})\le0$. Then

$$Q(\text{high,clean})-Q(\text{high,wait})\ge R_{clean}-R_{wait}>0,$$

so **clean** is strictly better — a contradiction. Hence **clean** is optimal at high. (Verified over a grid of $R_{clean},R_{wait}$.)

**(d)** The optimal action in low swings with the $R_{clean}/R_{wait}$ balance against the $-10$ empty penalty:

- **(i)** $R_{clean}=1000,R_{wait}=1$ → **clean**: the cleaning reward dwarfs both the $-10$ empty penalty and the tiny wait reward, so cleaning even from low is worth the empty risk.
- **(ii)** $R_{clean}=1,R_{wait}=0.99$ → **wait**: cleaning from low risks the $-10$ penalty for a reward barely above waiting, so it is safer to wait (collect $0.99$ indefinitely in low).
- **(iii)** $R_{clean}=1,R_{wait}=0.01$ → **charge**: waiting pays almost nothing and cleaning from low is too risky, so charge back to high (reward $0$ now) to earn cleaning rewards safely.

(All three confirmed by value iteration, $\gamma=0.9$.)

**💡 Trick.** For (c), argue by contradiction: if wait were optimal at high then $V^*(\text{high})=\frac{R_{wait}}{1-\gamma}$, but waiting forever from low forces $V^*(\text{low})\ge V^*(\text{high})$, making 

$$Q(\text{high,clean})-Q(\text{high,wait})\ge R_{clean}-R_{wait}>0$$

 — so clean wins.

**⚠️ Watch out.** In (d) the action in **low** is not monotone in a single parameter: it swings clean → wait → charge as the $R_{clean}/R_{wait}$ balance shifts against the $-10$ empty penalty. You must weigh the risk of reaching empty, not just compare $R_{clean}$ vs $R_{wait}$.

## Q2 (30 pts) — Value functions as a function of the reward: linearity vs. optimality
**Topics:** value function linearity in reward, optimal value monotonicity, subadditivity of $V^*$, policy stability | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_02, lecture_03
**Statement (English translation):**
Fix $S,A,s_0,p$. For a reward $r$, let $M_r=(S,A,p,s_0,r)$; $V_r^\pi(s)$ is the discounted return of $\pi$ from $s$ in $M_r$, and $V_r^*(s)$ that of the optimal policy. All rewards lie in $[0,1]$; $\gamma=0.9$. Given two different rewards $r_1,r_2$ and $r_3=r_1+r_2$. For each part determine the relation between the two expressions, from: **= (always equal)**, **≥ (first always ≥, and sometimes strictly >)**, **≤ (first always ≤, and sometimes strictly <)**, or **cannot be determined (sometimes >, sometimes <)**.

(a) $V_{r_3}^\pi$ vs $V_{r_1}^\pi+V_{r_2}^\pi$, for every policy $\pi$.

(b) $V_{r_3}^*$ vs $V_{r_1}^*+V_{r_2}^*$.

(c) $V_{r_3}^*$ vs $V_{r_1}^*$ (Hint: rewards are non-negative).

(d) Let $\pi_i^*$ be optimal for $r_i$. If $\pi_1^*(s)=\pi_2^*(s)=a$, must $\pi_3^*(s)=a$? Yes/No.

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** **= (equal).** For a *fixed* policy the return is linear in the reward,

$$V_r^\pi(s)=\mathbb{E}^\pi[\sum_t\gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)]$$

so $V_{r_1+r_2}^\pi=V_{r_1}^\pi+V_{r_2}^\pi$.

**(b)** **≤.** $V^*$ is sub-additive in the reward,

$$V_{r_3}^*=\max_\pi(V_{r_1}^\pi+V_{r_2}^\pi)\le\max_\pi V_{r_1}^\pi+\max_{\pi'}V_{r_2}^{\pi'}=V_{r_1}^*+V_{r_2}^*$$

so $V_{r_3}^*\le V_{r_1}^*+V_{r_2}^*$, strict when $r_1,r_2$ have different optimal policies.

**(c)** **≥.** Since $r_2\ge0$ we have $r_3=r_1+r_2\ge r_1$ pointwise, and $V^*$ is monotone in the reward,

$$r\le r'\Rightarrow V_r^\pi\le V_{r'}^\pi\ \forall\pi\Rightarrow V_r^*\le V_{r'}^*$$

so $V_{r_3}^*\ge V_{r_1}^*$, strict when $r_2>0$ on a reachable state.

**(d)** **No.** The optimal action need not be preserved under reward addition, because $V^*$ is not additive (part b). Concrete counterexample ($\gamma=0.9$): state $s$ has actions $a\!\to\!X$, $b\!\to\!Y$ (edge reward 0); $X$ leads to absorbing $A_1$ (pays $r_1=1$) or $A_2$ (pays $r_2=1$), and $Y$ leads to absorbing $B$ (pays $r_1=r_2=0.9$).

- Under $r_1$ alone: $Q(s,a)=8.1>Q(s,b)=7.29$, so $\pi_1^*(s)=a$; identically $\pi_2^*(s)=a$.
- Under $r_3=r_1+r_2$: at $X$ you must commit to one absorbing state paying $r_3=1$, so $V_{r_3}^*(X)=9$, whereas $B$ pays $r_3=1.8$, so $V_{r_3}^*(Y)=16.2$. Then $Q(s,a)=8.1<Q(s,b)=14.58$, so $\pi_3^*(s)=b\neq a$.

(python-verified.)

**💡 Trick.** Everything follows from one distinction: for a *fixed* $\pi$ the return is linear in $r$ (so (a) is exact equality), but $V^*=\max_\pi$ is only sub-additive (b, gives $\le$) and monotone in non-negative reward (c, gives $\ge$).

**⚠️ Watch out.** Don't assume shared optimal actions survive addition (d): because $V^*$ is not additive, $\pi_1^*(s)=\pi_2^*(s)=a$ does **not** force $\pi_3^*(s)=a$ — a single reachable state paying under $r_3$ can flip the argmax.

## Q3 (20 pts) — Logistic (sigmoid) two-action policy: score function and REINFORCE
**Topics:** parametric policy, sigmoid policy, score function, REINFORCE | **Pillar:** Approximation | **Difficulty:** 3
**Maps to:** lecture_09, lecture_10
**Statement (English translation):**
An MDP with two actions $A=\{0,1\}$. Each state $s$ has a feature vector $\phi(s)\in\mathbb{R}^d$ ($S$ finite); a policy is parameterized by $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d$ with
$$\pi(a=1\mid s;\theta)=\frac{1}{1+\exp(-\phi(s)\cdot\theta)}.$$

(a) Can this parameterization represent the policy that in every state picks each action w.p. $1/2$? (yes/no; give $\theta$ or a counterexample).

(b) Compute $\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a=1\mid s;\theta)$ and $\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a=0\mid s;\theta)$ (explain).

(c) Write the REINFORCE update at step $t$ as a function of a given trajectory $s_0,a_0,r_0,\dots,s_T,a_T,r_T$ (auxiliary variables allowed).

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** **Yes.** Take $\theta=\mathbf{0}$: then $\phi(s)\cdot\theta=0$ and $\pi(1\mid s)=\tfrac1{1+e^{0}}=\tfrac12$ for every $s$.

**(b)** Let $p:=\pi(1\mid s;\theta)=\sigma(\phi(s)\cdot\theta)$. Then $\nabla_\theta\log\pi(1\mid s;\theta)=(1-p)\,\phi(s)$ and $\nabla_\theta\log\pi(0\mid s;\theta)=-p\,\phi(s)$; compactly, for $a\in\{0,1\}$,

$$\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a\mid s;\theta)=(a-p)\,\phi(s)$$

(using $\sigma'=\sigma(1-\sigma)$).

**(c)** With return-to-go $G_t=\sum_{k=t}^{T}\gamma^{k-t}r_k$ and $p_t=\sigma(\phi(s_t)\cdot\theta)$,
$$\theta\leftarrow\theta+\eta_t\,\gamma^{t}\,G_t\,(a_t-p_t)\,\phi(s_t).$$
(Equivalently, the whole-trajectory sum 

$$\theta\leftarrow\theta+\eta\sum_{t}\gamma^t G_t(a_t-p_t)\phi(s_t)$$

.)

**💡 Trick.** Using $\sigma'=\sigma(1-\sigma)$, the score collapses to the single compact form $\nabla_\theta\log\pi(a\mid s;\theta)=(a-p)\,\phi(s)$ with $p=\sigma(\phi(s)\cdot\theta)$ — the "observed minus expected action" times the feature.

**⚠️ Watch out.** The sign of $(a-p)$ flips between $a=1$ and $a=0$. Write $(a_t-p_t)$, not $(1-p_t)$, in the REINFORCE update, and don't drop the $\gamma^t$ discount weighting each step.

## Q4 (20 pts) — "Max-outside-expectation" operator: contraction, and does its fixed point equal Q*?
**Topics:** contraction mapping, sup-norm, Bellman operator, Q*, max–expectation interchange | **Pillar:** Planning | **Difficulty:** 4
**Maps to:** lecture_03, lecture_02
**Statement (English translation):**
MDP $M=(S,A,p,s_0,r)$. For $U\in\mathbb{R}^{|S\times A|}$, $0<\gamma<1$, define
$$(T_M U)(s,a)=r(s,a)+\gamma\max_{a'}\ \mathbb{E}_{s'\sim p(\cdot\mid s,a)}\big[U(s',a')\big].$$

(a) State the definition of $H$ being $\gamma$-contracting in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$.

(b) Prove $T_M$ is $\gamma$-contracting in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$.

(c) Let $U^*$ be the fixed point of $T_M$. Does $U^*=Q^*$ hold for every MDP $M$? (yes/no; explain or give a counterexample).

**Solution sketch:**

**(a)** $H$ is $\gamma$-contracting in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ if

$$\|HU-HV\|_\infty\le\gamma\|U-V\|_\infty$$

for all $U,V$.

**(b)** The $r(s,a)$ terms cancel, so

$$\begin{aligned}|(T_MU-T_MV)(s,a)|&=\gamma\,|\max_{a'}\mathbb{E}_{s'}U(s',a')-\max_{a'}\mathbb{E}_{s'}V(s',a')|\\&\le\gamma\max_{a'}|\mathbb{E}_{s'}[U(s',a')-V(s',a')]|\\&\le\gamma\max_{a'}\mathbb{E}_{s'}|U(s',a')-V(s',a')|\\&\le\gamma\|U-V\|_\infty\end{aligned}$$

(using $|\max f-\max g|\le\max|f-g|$ and Jensen/monotonicity of $\mathbb{E}$). Take the max over $(s,a)$.

**(c)** **No.** $T_M$ takes the max **outside** the expectation, so

$$\begin{aligned}U^*(s,a)&=r(s,a)+\gamma\max_{a'}\mathbb{E}_{s'}[U^*(s',a')],\\ Q^*(s,a)&=r(s,a)+\gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s'}[\max_{a'}Q^*(s',a')].\end{aligned}$$

with 

$$\max_{a'}\mathbb{E}_{s'}[\cdot]\le\mathbb{E}_{s'}[\max_{a'}\cdot]$$

. $T_M$ must commit to one action $a'$ for all realized next states, so $U^*\le Q^*$, strictly when the best next action depends on the realized $s'$. Counterexample ($\gamma=0.9$): from $(s_0,a)$ jump to $s_1$ or $s_2$ each w.p. $1/2$. At $s_1$ action 1 pays 1 (action 2 pays 0), at $s_2$ action 2 pays 1 (action 1 pays 0), both absorbing. Then $Q^*(s_0,a)=\gamma\cdot\tfrac{1}{1-\gamma}=9$, but 

$$U^*(s_0,a)=\gamma\cdot\tfrac{1+\gamma}{2(1-\gamma)}=8.55<9$$

. (python-verified.) They agree only for deterministic transitions or when one $a'$ is simultaneously optimal at every reachable next state.

**💡 Trick.** The whole question turns on the interchange 

$$\max_{a'}\mathbb{E}_{s'}[\cdot]\le\mathbb{E}_{s'}[\max_{a'}\cdot]$$

: $T_M$ must commit to one $a'$ before seeing $s'$, whereas $Q^*$ picks $a'$ after, so $U^*\le Q^*$ with equality only when the best $a'$ is the same at every reachable $s'$.

**⚠️ Watch out.** The contraction proof still goes through (both use $|\max f-\max g|\le\max|f-g|$ plus $|\mathbb{E}|\le\mathbb{E}|\cdot|$), so being a valid contraction does **not** mean $U^*=Q^*$ — a stochastic transition where the best next action depends on $s'$ makes the inequality strict.
